# Small Business Launch SOP: From Idea to First Dollar

**Generic Student Version | Human-Readable Training Guide**  
**Prepared:** May 2026  
**Use case:** Small business startup, local service business, consulting business, online business, and first-dollar execution.

---

## Read This First

This SOP is built for action. The goal is to move from "I have an idea" to "I can legally take money, deliver work, invoice, get paid, and stay compliant."

Work through the phases in order:

1. Protect the business.
2. Separate the money.
3. Get legal to operate.
4. Set up payment and paperwork.
5. Sell one clear offer.
6. Improve after real customers respond.

This is operational guidance, not legal or tax advice. Students should speak with a CPA, attorney, insurance broker, or local business advisor before making tax elections, signing large contracts, hiring employees, or entering regulated industries.

---

## Important 2026 Compliance Notes

Some rules in older startup guides have changed.

- U.S.-formed companies are currently exempt from FinCEN BOI reporting requirements. Foreign entities registered to do business in the U.S. may still have BOI duties. Source: [FinCEN BOI guidance](https://www.fincen.gov/boi).
- Federal contracting uses the SAM.gov Unique Entity ID, not DUNS, as the official federal award identifier. Source: [GSA Unique Entity ID guidance](https://www.gsa.gov/node/124286).
- California LLC Statements of Information are due within 90 days after formation, then every two years. Source: [California Secretary of State FAQ](https://www.sos.ca.gov/business-programs/business-entities/faqs).
- California LLCs generally owe an $800 annual tax if registered with the Secretary of State or doing business in California. Source: [California LLC tax guidance](https://taxes.ca.gov/income-tax/limited-liability-companies/).

## The First-Dollar Roadmap

| Phase | Outcome | What "done" looks like |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Legal foundation | The business exists and has basic protection | Entity selected, EIN saved, operating agreement created if using an LLC |
| 2. Money foundation | Business money is separate | Bank account open, bookkeeping started, receipts captured |
| 3. Compliance | The business is allowed to operate | Local license, industry permits, insurance, tax calendar |
| 4. Sales setup | Customers can understand and buy | Offer, online presence, contract, invoice, payment method |
| 5. First-dollar sprint | Revenue begins | 25-50 targeted leads contacted, first invoice sent, first payment received |
| 6. Improve | The business becomes repeatable | Customer feedback, refined offer, cleaner process |

## Phase 1: Legal Foundation

### Step 1: Pick the right structure

Most small businesses begin as either a sole proprietorship or an LLC. The better choice depends on risk, cost, and seriousness of the business.

| Structure | Best use | Watch-out |
|---|---|---|
| Sole proprietor | Testing a low-risk side business | No liability wall between personal and business assets |
| Single-member LLC | Most serious solo businesses | Must keep money and records separate |
| Multi-member LLC | Two or more owners | Needs clear ownership, voting, exit, and profit rules |
| LLC taxed as S-Corp | Businesses with stable profit, often above about $40K net/year | Requires payroll, extra tax filings, and reasonable salary |
| C-Corp | Venture-backed startup path | More complexity and potential double taxation |

**Student takeaway:** The structure should match the risk. A low-risk weekend service may start simple. A business with vehicles, employees, contracts, customer property, software liability, or physical locations needs stronger protection.

References:

- [IRS Business Structures](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/business-structures)
- [SBA Choose a Business Structure](https://www.sba.gov/business-guide/launch-your-business/choose-business-structure)

### Step 2: Form the business correctly

For a California LLC, the common formation steps are:

1. Search the business name.
2. File Articles of Organization.
3. Appoint a registered agent.
4. Create an Operating Agreement.
5. Apply for an EIN.
6. File the first Statement of Information within 90 days.
7. Set reminders for taxes, renewals, and future filings.

Core records to save:

- Articles of Organization
- EIN confirmation letter
- Operating Agreement
- Statement of Information
- Registered agent information
- Tax and licensing records

References:

- [CA BizFile Online](https://bizfileonline.sos.ca.gov/)
- [CA Business Entity Search](https://bizfileonline.sos.ca.gov/search/business)
- [CA Secretary of State FAQ](https://www.sos.ca.gov/business-programs/business-entities/faqs)

### Step 3: Get and save the EIN

The EIN is the business tax ID. It is needed for banking, taxes, insurance, payroll, vendor accounts, and many business applications.

Action checklist:

- Apply directly through the IRS.
- Save the EIN confirmation letter as a PDF.
- Store it in the business admin folder.
- Use the EIN for business accounts whenever appropriate.

Reference:

- [IRS Apply for EIN](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online)

### Step 4: Use an Operating Agreement

An Operating Agreement explains how the LLC is managed. Single-owner businesses should still have one because it supports the separation between the owner and the company.

Minimum sections:

- Owner names and ownership percentages
- Management authority
- Business purpose
- Capital contributions
- Banking and accounting rules
- Profit distributions
- Voting rules, if more than one owner
- Exit and dissolution procedures
- Signature and date

## Phase 2: Money Foundation

### Step 5: Open a business bank account

Never mix business and personal money. This protects liability separation, makes taxes cleaner, and creates a financial record for lenders, partners, and customers.

Typical documents:

- EIN letter
- Formation documents
- Operating Agreement
- Photo ID
- Business address and phone
- Initial deposit, if required

Banking options to compare:

| Option | Best for |
|---|---|
| Local credit union | Local service businesses and relationship banking |
| Major bank | Branch access, merchant services, lending relationship |
| Online business bank | Low fees, modern tools, fast setup |
| Industry-specific platform | Startups, e-commerce, or payment-heavy businesses |

References:

- [Chase Business Checking](https://www.chase.com/business/banking/checking)
- [Bank of America Small Business](https://smallbusiness.bankofamerica.com/)
- [Mercury](https://mercury.com/)
- [Relay](https://relayfi.com/)

### Step 6: Start bookkeeping before the first sale

Bookkeeping starts when money starts moving, not at tax time.

Weekly habit:

- Categorize every bank transaction.
- Upload receipts.
- Reconcile bank balance to accounting software.
- Check unpaid invoices.
- Review cash on hand.

Common tools:

| Tool | Best for |
|---|---|
| QuickBooks Online | Most small businesses and CPA workflows |
| Wave | Very early-stage businesses with simple needs |
| Xero | Growing businesses that want clean reporting |
| FreshBooks | Service businesses that invoice frequently |
| Zoho Books | Lower-cost operations with CRM needs |

References:

- [QuickBooks Online](https://quickbooks.intuit.com/)
- [Wave](https://www.waveapps.com/)
- [Xero](https://www.xero.com/)

### Step 7: Build business credit carefully

Business credit can help a company access better vendor terms, cards, and financing. It does not replace sales.

Starter sequence:

1. Open a business bank account.
2. Create a complete business identity: name, address, phone, email, website.
3. Get a D&B profile if useful for vendors.
4. Open 2-3 starter Net-30 accounts only for items the business actually needs.
5. Pay invoices early.
6. Monitor business credit reports.
7. Apply for a business card after the business has activity.

Starter vendors to evaluate:

- [Uline](https://www.uline.com/BL_8542/Open-Account)
- [Quill](https://www.quill.com/help/customer-service/credit-application.html)
- [Grainger](https://www.grainger.com/content/credit-application)
- [NAV Business Credit Monitor](https://www.nav.com/)
- [D&B DUNS Number](https://www.dnb.com/duns-number/get-a-duns-number.html)

## Phase 3: Licenses, Insurance, and Compliance

### Step 8: Get local permission to operate

State formation does not automatically authorize local business activity. Cities and counties may require a business license, business tax certificate, zoning clearance, home occupation permit, health permit, professional license, or industry-specific approval.

Start here:

- [CalGold Permit Search](https://www.calgold.ca.gov/)
- Local city business license office
- County business resources
- State licensing board for regulated industries

Examples:

| Business type | Possible extra requirements |
|---|---|
| Food business | Health permit, food handler certification, commercial kitchen rules |
| Automotive repair | Automotive repair dealer registration and related compliance |
| Construction/trades | Contractor license, bond, insurance |
| Childcare | State licensing and background checks |
| Professional services | Professional license, E&O insurance |
| E-commerce with physical goods | Seller's permit and sales tax collection |

### Step 9: Buy the right insurance

Insurance protects the business and is often required by landlords, clients, lenders, and contracts.

| Coverage | Needed for |
|---|---|
| General Liability | Most businesses |
| Professional Liability / E&O | Advice, consulting, design, software, technical services |
| Commercial Auto | Vehicles used for business |
| Workers' Compensation | Employees, subject to state law |
| Cyber Liability | Customer data, online systems, payment data, SaaS |
| Commercial Property | Equipment, inventory, office, shop, or leased space |
| Industry-specific coverage | Customer vehicles, food liability, tools, events, health services, etc. |

Quote sources:

- [Next Insurance](https://www.nextinsurance.com/)
- [Hiscox](https://www.hiscox.com/small-business-insurance)
- [Progressive Commercial](https://www.progressivecommercial.com/)
- [State Fund California](https://www.statefund.com/)

### Step 10: Get a seller's permit when selling physical goods

A California seller's permit is generally required when selling tangible personal property in California.

Likely triggers:

- Products
- Parts
- Merchandise
- Equipment
- Bundled physical goods

Usually not triggered by:

- Pure consulting
- Pure SaaS
- Some labor-only services, depending on the transaction

Reference:

- [CDTFA Permits and Licenses](https://www.cdtfa.ca.gov/services/permits-licenses.html)

## Phase 4: Optional Government Contracting Path

Government contracting is optional. It can be powerful for the right business, but it adds paperwork, longer sales cycles, and stricter compliance.

### Step 11: Register in SAM.gov if pursuing federal work

SAM.gov is the gateway for federal contracts, grants, and many subcontracting opportunities.

SAM produces:

- Unique Entity ID (UEI)
- CAGE code, when registration is completed and validated
- Entity record visible to contracting officers
- Representations and certifications

What to prepare:

- Legal business name exactly matching IRS records
- EIN
- Physical address
- Bank account for electronic payment
- NAICS codes
- Points of contact
- Capability summary

Reference:

- [SAM.gov](https://sam.gov/)

### Step 12: Consider certifications only when they fit

Certifications can help a qualified business compete for certain contracts, but they do not replace sales, performance, pricing, or relationships.

Common certification categories:

- Small business
- Women-owned small business
- Veteran-owned small business
- Service-disabled veteran-owned small business
- Minority-owned business
- Disadvantaged business enterprise
- HUBZone business
- State or local supplier diversity certification

Action sequence:

1. Confirm eligibility.
2. Gather ownership and control documents.
3. Register in the required portal.
4. Update the website and capability statement after approval.
5. Build relationships with buyers and prime contractors.

References:

- [SBA Contracting Assistance Programs](https://www.sba.gov/federal-contracting/contracting-assistance-programs)
- [USASpending.gov](https://www.usaspending.gov/)
- [Grants.gov](https://www.grants.gov/)

### Step 13: Build a one-page capability statement

A capability statement is a one-page sales sheet for government and corporate buyers.

Include:

- Company name, logo, and contact information
- Core competencies
- Differentiators
- Past performance or relevant experience
- NAICS codes, if pursuing government work
- UEI and CAGE code, if registered in SAM
- Certifications, if applicable
- Insurance status, if useful

## Phase 5: Sales, Contracts, Invoicing, and Getting Paid

### Step 14: Define one sellable offer

A business does not need a giant menu to earn the first dollar. It needs one clear offer that solves one painful problem for one specific customer.

Use this format:

> We help [specific customer] solve [specific problem] by delivering [specific service/product] with [clear outcome].

Examples:

- "We help local homeowners keep their lawns maintained with weekly yard service and simple monthly pricing."
- "We help small restaurants improve their Google presence with profile optimization, photos, and review-request systems."
- "We help independent contractors organize receipts and invoices so tax season is less stressful."
- "We help small retailers create simple inventory spreadsheets that show what is selling and what is sitting."

### Step 15: Make the business findable

Minimum digital presence:

- Google Business Profile
- Simple website or landing page
- Professional email
- Social media or LinkedIn page, depending on the customer
- Basic service list
- Contact form and phone number

Minimum website pages:

1. Home
2. Services
3. About
4. Proof or credentials
5. Contact

References:

- [Google Business Profile](https://business.google.com/)
- [Google Workspace](https://workspace.google.com/)

### Step 16: Use contracts before work starts

Every job should have written terms. A contract does not need to be complicated; it needs to be clear.

Minimum contract sections:

- Parties
- Scope of work
- Deliverables
- Timeline
- Price
- Payment terms
- Change-order process
- Client responsibilities
- Limitation of liability
- Confidentiality, if needed
- Termination
- Governing law
- Signatures

Practical payment terms:

- Small service job: due at completion or Net-15.
- Consulting project: 50% upfront, 50% at delivery.
- Larger B2B contract: milestone payments or Net-30.

E-signature tools:

- [DocuSign](https://www.docusign.com/)
- [Dropbox Sign](https://sign.dropbox.com/)
- [PandaDoc](https://www.pandadoc.com/)

### Step 17: Send invoices the same day

Every invoice should include:

- Business legal name
- Address, phone, and email
- Invoice number
- Invoice date
- Due date
- Customer name
- Itemized work
- Payment methods
- Late fee terms, if included in the signed agreement

Payment options:

- ACH transfer
- Stripe
- Square
- Zelle Business, if available through the bank
- Check

References:

- [Stripe](https://stripe.com/)
- [Square](https://squareup.com/)

## Phase 6: Taxes and Calendar Discipline

### Step 18: Plan for quarterly estimated taxes

Self-employed owners often need to make quarterly tax payments. Missing payments can create penalties even if the annual return is filed correctly.

Typical federal quarterly dates:

| Quarter | Income period | Due date |
|---|---|---|
| Q1 | Jan 1-Mar 31 | April 15 |
| Q2 | Apr 1-May 31 | June 15 |
| Q3 | Jun 1-Aug 31 | September 15 |
| Q4 | Sep 1-Dec 31 | January 15 of following year |

Pay online:

- [IRS Direct Pay](https://www.irs.gov/payments/direct-pay)
- [California FTB Web Pay](https://www.ftb.ca.gov/pay/)

### Step 19: Understand state-level costs

Each state has its own taxes, fees, renewals, and filing rules. Students should check the rules for the state where the business is formed and where it operates.

California example:

- LLC annual tax is generally $800.
- Annual tax is due on the 15th day of the fourth month after the beginning of the tax year.
- Additional LLC fee applies when California total income reaches $250,000 or more.
- LLC Statements of Information are filed every two years after the initial filing.

Reference:

- [California LLC Tax Guidance](https://taxes.ca.gov/income-tax/limited-liability-companies/)

### Step 20: Keep a monthly compliance rhythm

Monthly:

- Reconcile books.
- Save receipts.
- Review unpaid invoices.
- Review tax savings account.
- Check upcoming renewals.

Quarterly:

- Pay estimated taxes if required.
- Review insurance needs.
- Review sales pipeline.
- Update business credit records, if building credit.

Annually or by anniversary:

- State LLC/corporation filings.
- Business tax return.
- Business license renewal.
- Insurance renewal.
- Registered agent renewal, if using a service.
- 1099-NEC filing for contractors paid $600+.
- Payroll filings, if employees exist.

## Student Action Plan

### Highest priority: next 7 days

- Choose one business idea to test.
- Define the customer and problem.
- Decide whether the business can start as a simple test or needs an LLC immediately.
- Search the business name.
- Create a basic business folder for legal, tax, banking, insurance, contracts, and marketing.
- Draft one sellable offer.

### Next 30 days

- Form the entity if appropriate.
- Apply for EIN if needed.
- Open a business bank account.
- Set up basic bookkeeping.
- Check local licenses and permits.
- Get insurance quotes if the business has customer, vehicle, property, physical, or professional risk.
- Create a simple website or landing page.
- Create invoice and agreement templates.
- Build a first prospect list of 50 potential customers.

### Next 60 days

- Contact the first 25 prospects.
- Send follow-ups.
- Hold discovery conversations.
- Close one paid first step.
- Deliver the work.
- Ask for feedback and testimonial.
- Improve the offer based on what customers actually cared about.

### Next 90 days

- Contact at least 50 total prospects.
- Track conversion numbers.
- Refine pricing.
- Clean up bookkeeping.
- Review tax obligations.
- Decide whether to continue, pivot, or stop the idea.

### Six-month targets

- First paying customer.
- One repeatable offer.
- Basic legal and financial foundation complete.
- Clean books through the current month.
- At least one testimonial, case example, or referral.
- Clear decision on whether the business is worth scaling.

## First-Dollar Sprint

Use this when the business has enough foundation to sell.

### Day 1: Pick the offer

Write:

- Who it is for
- What problem it solves
- What is included
- What is not included
- Price or starting price
- Delivery timeline

### Day 2: Build the proof packet

Create:

- One-page offer sheet
- Short email pitch
- Simple intake form
- Invoice template
- Agreement template

### Day 3: Make the prospect list

Find 50 prospects:

- Local businesses
- Neighbors or community members
- Industry contacts
- LinkedIn contacts
- Former employers or coworkers
- Student networks
- Local organizations
- Online groups where the customer already spends time

### Days 4-10: Outreach

Daily minimum:

- 5 direct emails or messages
- 5 phone calls, visits, or direct conversations
- 1 follow-up to anyone who replied

Track:

- Contact name
- Company or organization
- Role
- Email or phone
- Date contacted
- Response
- Next action

### Days 11-14: Close the first paid job

When someone shows interest:

1. Ask what problem they are trying to solve.
2. Confirm timeline and urgency.
3. Offer a simple paid first step.
4. Send agreement.
5. Send invoice or payment link.
6. Deliver quickly.
7. Ask for testimonial, referral, or next project.
